The challenges

C1 — First Blood

⭐ Warm-up · 50 pts

Identify an unknown hash, then crack it with a wordlist.

  • (−5) "Identify before you crack."
  • (−10) "It's MD5, 32 hex chars."
  • (−15) "The password is the most clichéd one there is."

C1B — Hidden in Plain Sight

⭐ Warm-up · 25 pts

This one looks scrambled but it's a reversible encoding, not a one-way hash. You don't crack it — you decode it.

  • (−5) "This isn't a hash — notice the == at the end."
  • (−10) "It's Base64."
  • (−15) "Decode it with base64 -d."

C2 — Old Faithful

⭐ Warm-up · 50 pts

Not everything is MD5. Count the characters and choose the right format/mode.

  • (−5) "Count the characters: 40, not 32."
  • (−10) "SHA-1."
  • (−15) "A very loving 2009 password."

C2B — Cindy's Clever Trick

⭐⭐ Easy · 75 pts

Story: "Cindy's former password was compromised, so she added an !1 to it, thinking it was smart." A plain wordlist won't match a word she changed — you have to mutate each candidate (hybrid or a rule).

  • (−5) "Read c2b_README.txt."
  • (−10) "Her old password is common — she just glued !1 on the end."
  • (−15) "Hybrid: append !1 to each rockyou word."

C3 — Season's Greetings

⭐⭐ Easy · 100 pts

Plain rockyou misses it. Rules mutate words into real-world variants (capital + year suffix).

  • (−10) "The base word is a season."
  • (−15) "Capital letter + a recent year."
  • (−20) "Apply class.rule (or best64)."

C4 — No Dictionary Here

⭐⭐⭐ Medium · 150 pts

This password isn't in any wordlist — brute force a known shape with a mask.

  • (−10) "No wordlist will contain this. Think structure."
  • (−20) "4 characters."
  • (−30) "Mask ?u?l?d?l."

C5 — Insider Knowledge

⭐⭐⭐ Medium · 200 pts

The password is company jargon. Build a custom wordlist from the target's own words (CeWL), then hybrid it. The company intranet is served at http://127.0.0.1:8000/intranet.html inside the container.

  • (−15) "Generic lists fail; the password is company jargon."
  • (−25) "Spider the local intranet page with CeWL."
  • (−35) "CeWL → custom list, then append four digits."

C6 — Front Door

⭐⭐⭐ Medium · 150 pts

This isn't a hash — it's a live SSH login. Online guessing with Hydra against a service you own (ssh://vuln-login). Notice how slow and noisy it is vs. offline cracking.

  • (−10) "This isn't a hash — it's a live login."
  • (−20) "User is labuser."
  • (−30) "Hydra + a tiny wordlist over SSH."

C7 — Slow Burn

⭐⭐⭐⭐ Hard · 250 pts

Same rockyou attack as C1, but against a slow hash. Feel why key stretching changes everything — compare your hashes/sec to C1.

  • (−20) "$2b$ = bcrypt."
  • (−30) "Same wordlist as C1 — just be patient; that's the point."
  • (−40) "hashcat -m 3200."

C8 — Windows Pivot

⭐⭐⭐⭐ Hard · 250 pts

32 hex chars, but it's not MD5 — it's how Windows stores passwords (NTLM). Unsalted ⇒ identical passwords produce identical hashes, and it cracks fast. Crack with rockyou + rules.

  • (−20) "32 hex chars but NOT MD5 — it's how Windows stores passwords."
  • (−30) "NTLM, hashcat mode 1000 / john --format=nt."
  • (−40) "rockyou + class.rule (capitalize + append digits)."

C9 — Dillon's Cookie Shop

⭐⭐⭐ Bonus · 200 pts · cookies.0byte.sh

A cute bakery site with a deliberately weak staff login. Brute-force the HTTP POST form with Hydra (http[s]-post-form), then log in to reveal the recipe and the flag. The failure message Invalid credentials is your marker. From the toolbox: docker compose run --rm crackstation.

  • "Username is dillon."
  • "The password is a cookie-themed dictionary word — try the bundled cookie-wordlist.txt."
  • "Form posts to /login with fields username / password; failure marker is Invalid credentials."

🎁 Defender's Gambit

Bonus · 100 pts · write-up

Flip attacker → defender. In a few sentences, name the defense that beats each attack you used (fast-hash dump, rules/dictionary, mask, custom wordlist, online guessing, NTLM). Cite one NIST or OWASP point. Judged by the instructor.

Max base points: 1500 (C1–C9 + C1B + C2B). Plus the Defender's Gambit bonus (+100).
